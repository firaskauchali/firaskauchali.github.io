  • arthur erickson

    The 20th-century modernist, Arthur Erickson, was a legendary Canadian architect and the first to gain international attention in Canadian architectural history. He gained popularity for his ability to mold structures to their natural landscapes in both urban, and rural environments.

    ‘Arthur Erickson: unifying concrete with nature’ is a 12-page artist monograph showcasing just a couple of the iconic building he had created during a career lasting over 40 years. The objective was to ensure the booklet reinforces the expression of the architect by referencing the elements found in Erickson’s work.

    Erickson is one of the very few brutalist architects who was praised for his sculptural use of concrete; he was often referred to as “the concrete poet.” During his trips to Japan, he was inspired by how Japanese architects, specifically Tadao Ando, were able to apply concrete as a much finer finish.

    Concrete is the marble of the 20th century

    After exploring with a bunch of typefaces, I quickly found that Berthold’s Akzidenz Grotesk married beautifully with Erickson’s modernist style. I decided to stick within that family, and utilize various weights and sizes to create hierarchy. I don’t think it gets more brutal than Akzidenz Grotesk Super.

    The most difficult part was deciding what I should feature in this incredibly short booklet. I decided to limit myself to just two of Erickson’s buildings that I believe represent his gift of being able to juxtapose concrete with nature.

    I knew, in order to truly display Erickson’s work, I would need to capture the unapologetic strength of brutalism while also paying close attention to balancing the relationships and space between each element on the spread. I got the character styles down pretty easily, but nailing the layout for each spread took an extensive amount of exploration and refinement.

    I came up with the idea of having Erickson’s name embossed on the cover midway through the project. Printing his name by molding it with the landscape it sits on—or in this case, the paper—is, in my opinon, an excellent way to commemorate his life’s work. However, there was one big issue with this process. It was unreasonably expensive to have a custom design embossed professionally for the amount of copies I was printing

    I was so settled on this idea that I decided to give it a try myself, using the tools and materials I had access to. I refined the typography by tweaking a few characters so they aligned with each other harmoniously. When I was happy with the design, I laser cut the characters and created a plate with some cheap illustration board. I chose Stonehenge Paper in Steel Grey to mimic concrete and began testing my handcrafted plate with basic ceramic tools and some elbow grease, and this is what I got …

    … yeah, didn't really turn out the way I imagined. Before completely scrapping the idea, I ran the plate through an etching press using the same technique one would when making an intaglio print (without the ink) and voila! It came out a lot better than I could have hoped for.

  • don’t cry

    The objective for this project was to explore methods of creating typography by using found or constructed materials that can be found in our everyday environment. It was fairly simply—we were to choose a word or phrase and draw it out using objects that visually represent what we’re trying to communicate.

    During the same time I was coming up with concepts for this project, I was—for some odd reason—intrigued by our use of idioms. They’re so unusual; they can’t be understood in a literal sense, but we pick them up so naturally and they metaphorically paint a picture that can only be interpreted by people who speak the same language or share the same culture. So I made a long list of all the idioms I could think of and tried coming up with clever concepts to communicate them through found typography.

    The idiom, “Don't cry over spilled milk,” resonates with me and I believe it was the strongest concept that I had thought of for this project. Even though I’m only displaying the first two words of the phrase, the visual cues were more than enough to communicate what I am getting at. Hinting at the phrase allows the audience to have an interactive expierence with the piece. It creates the “aha!” moment which we all love—the same one you probably had earlier on as you looked through this project.

    To create the typographic piece itself, I limited myself to only use a glass of 2% milk and a couple straws. As I was creating the piece I thought, “It definitely would have been much easier to use white school glue and a brush,” but that wouldn’t be fair—it would lose its integrity. As you can see, the first attempt was a disaster.

    I knew there was no point in crying over spilled milk so I just cleaned up and gave it another shot. It seemed to be working well this time, but I wasn’t exactly satisfied with the direction, especially the letter forms.

    Finding an appropriate art direction was tough. The most difficult aspect was making sure my aesthetic choices were synonymous with the message being conveyed. To accomplish this, I switched the red background to a dark wooden surface, creating a more dramatic scene. I also inversed and cleaned up the typography. The type was influenced by the letter forms plastered on the walls of The Korova Milk Bar from the film, A Clockwork Orange.

    Third time’s the charm.

  • dabbah wallah

    Dabbah Wallah is a North American mithai shop. I conceptualized the visual identity, product and packaging design for this brand as my final project for Package Design. The objective was to create a product that communicates effectively with two very different cultures. The modular packaging is constructed by precisely cutting cardstock without the use of adhesives.

    Mithai, or Indian sweets, are a huge part of India’s culture. They are these incredibly sweet, fried desserts that usually contain sugar, milk, or condensed milk as base ingredients. The rest of the ingredients vary according to the palette of that specific region. Mithai are commonly shared during festivals such as Holi, Diwali, Eid, and Raksha Bhandan. Although mithai are a rich part of Indian culture, I haven’t seen that translate well into the North American market.

    I began by researching how sweets are made, presented, and sold in India. I realized that a lot of effort goes into the product, the mithai themselves, and not the brand. Mithai are usually found at small bakeries or street vendors. They go by the pound and are usually packaged in paper bags or boxes. In a country with thousands of flavours, every sweet vendor adds a slightly different taste to make their product stand out. There isn’t much effort put into the presentation or brand experience—these luxuries just don’t matter to the customer, who are usually just locals of the area.

    In North America, we have a completely different sense of consumer culture. By looking at the differences, I’ve created an experience that is suitable for both the Indian community—who are familiar with the product—and the unfamiliar faces who will be trying mithai for the first time. Since we’re marketing for both parties, it is important that we sustain enough of India’s culture while slightly tweaking areas in order to appeal to the western market.

    The solution was to simply combine some of the heavily cultured aspects of India and incorporate that into the brand. “Dabbah Wallah” roots from the Hindi title of a lunch box delivery worker. The workers regularly deliver hot lunches in tiffin boxes (lunch boxes) to their clients. I took the concept and visual form of the tiffin box and implemented it into the brand’s visual language.

    Tiffin boxes are very common in India as most people prefer to eat home cooked meals over fast food. The tiffin box has multiple compartments so that the user can carry different dishes while keeping them separated. It’s very uncommon for a customer to purchase just one box of mithai since they’re usually shared with family and friends. By putting the two together, I translated the tiffin box into the packaging for Dabbah Wallah to satisfy the needs of the customer while also enhancing the cultural aspect of the brand.

    The hexagonal shape of the box, the patterns, colours, and overall visual design is inspired from India’s folk art. A common decorational art, known as Rangoli, can be seen everywhere during any of the festivals held in India. The pattern is formed and repeated in a circular path to create an intricate design. Made from a variety of coloured powder, Rangoli patterns are very vibrant. All of these elements were taken into consideration when developing the visual identity of the brand. In order to satisfy the rest of our audience, the patterns have been simplified and the colour scheme includes a more diverse set of hues.

    Extreme Weather

    The brief for this project is to develop a strategy for a specific audience in order to help them understand and prepare for an extreme weather emergency. The campaign deliverables have to be bilingual and specific to the city of Toronto. The focus isn’t to change behaviours that impact environment, but to create a community discussion, involvement, and resiliency to reduce vunerability in the event of a weather emergency. The goal here is to strengthen our immediate communitites.

    My target audience are young adults, specifically millenials, due to the potential impact they can bring through various methods of communication including social media. Young Canadians have proven their trivial influence in the recent election where Liberals won majority.

    The first deliverable is a poster which will attract the attention of young adults in Toronto and create a conversation about extreme weather preparedness. I chose to create a system for a series of posters comparing emergency preparedness to the simple tasks we perform on a regular basis in order to avoid common difficulties. The advertisment is intially absorbed with a sense of humour in order to capture the audience, and then relays a hard hitting message which will hopefully resonate with them.

    Strolling Magazine

    I’m currently working on a magazine for Editorial Design. We are to conceive a completely new magazine, including the identity, format, and genre.

    Strolling magazine aims to stimulate young adults with an intriguing view on societal culture. The magazine uses photojournalism, literature, and experimental typography to capture the essence of the city featured in the issue. Unlike news journalism which discusses live events, Strolling investigates cultural realities that make up society.

    The idea of strolling derives from the term Flânerie, the act of strolling. According to Charles Baudelaire, who coined the term, the flâneur is “a botanist of the sidewalk”. They stroll the city in order to experience the dynamics of the cty from a disengaged point of view. Another aesthetically attuned observer is the street photographer. All photographs showcased throughout the magazine are captured through black & white analog photography, with little to no re-touching. This beta issue will be featuring the city of Toronto.

    I'm still experimenting with the aesthetic of the magazine. This is my most current grid structure and some sample spreads. I am also considering printing the magazine on a risograph to add some colour to the b&w photographs.
